OVERVIEW

The Challenge

City of Sartell employees spend significant time searching through documents for policy and ordinance information. Field staff call back to city hall for answers, creating interruptions. Information retrieval is slow, particularly for mobile workers. As the city explores AI, the opportunity extends beyond a single tool: a connected system that serves employees internally, residents externally, and callers by phone, all drawing from the same trusted knowledge base.

The Approach: One Foundation, Multiple Interfaces

This project is built on a single, shared foundation. Every phase shares the same components:

  • One knowledge base. The SharePoint document library built in Phase 1 is the single source of truth. Every phase adds to it or reads from it. Nothing is rebuilt or duplicated.
  • One search engine. The indexing, chunking, and semantic search set up in Phase 1 powers every interface that follows.
  • One AI model. The same language model processes queries whether they come from a Teams chat, a website widget, or a phone call.
  • One conversation design standard. Accuracy, citation behavior, escalation logic, and tone carry across all phases.

What changes between phases is the audience, the interface, and the access controls. Internal employees get department-specific language. Residents get plain language. Voice callers get concise spoken responses. But the brain behind all of it is the same.

This means work done in one phase carries forward to the next. The system gets smarter and more capable over time as each phase builds on what came before.

Phase 2: Internal Knowledge Base Build-Out

Discover, audit, and onboard internal city documents (HR policies, department procedures, administrative guidelines) into the AI system.

View full details →

A dedicated engagement to discover, audit, organize, and onboard the city's internal documents into the AI system. This transforms the bot from a public-information tool into a comprehensive internal knowledge assistant covering HR policies, department procedures, administrative guidelines, and operational documents.

Why This Is a Separate Phase

Phase 1 uses publicly available website content that is already published, organized, and considered final. Phase 2 involves fundamentally different work:

  • Discovery and stakeholder coordination. Meeting with each department head to identify what documents exist, where they live, and what gaps need to be flagged.
  • Content audit and readiness assessment. Assessing internal documents for accuracy, relevance, and inclusion.
  • Information architecture. Designing the SharePoint structure for internal documents with proper metadata, categorization, and access controls.
  • Bot retraining and conversation tuning. New topic areas need testing. The bot needs to handle questions spanning both public and internal content.
  • Validation and testing. Each department's content tested with real queries before rollout.

Candidate Document Categories

  • HR and Employee Resources (high priority). Employee handbook, benefits information, onboarding procedures, and time-off policies.
  • Administrative Policies and Procedures. Purchasing and procurement, travel and reimbursement, and acceptable use policies.
  • Department-Specific Content. Brand guide, inspection checklists, standard operating procedures, and internal finance procedures.

Deliverables

  • Department-by-department content audit
  • Organized SharePoint document library with access controls
  • Updated bot with expanded knowledge base
  • Testing and validation across all new content areas
  • Documentation of gaps (documents departments need but don't yet have)

Estimated investment: $27,000

Phase 3: External AI Chatbot (Website)

Public-facing chatbot on sartellmn.com for residents, visitors, and businesses.

View full details →

A public-facing version of the chatbot deployed on sartellmn.com. Residents, visitors, and businesses ask questions and get answers drawn from the city's published information.

Why Public-Facing Changes Everything

When an internal bot gives a bad answer, an employee corrects it and moves on. When a public-facing bot gives a bad answer, it is the City of Sartell that said it. That answer could end up in a council meeting, on social media, or in local news. The public bot also introduces legal and compliance considerations that the internal bot does not: accessibility requirements, FOIA implications for conversations with residents, and the need for clear boundaries on topics the bot should not touch. The technical foundation from Phase 1 carries forward, but the conversation design, testing, and risk management are substantially different work.

Who uses it: Residents, visitors, businesses, anyone on the city website.

Scope

  • Website integration and deployment. Embedded chat widget or standalone page on sartellmn.com, coordinated with the city's website platform (Revize). Includes mobile responsive design, cross-browser testing, and ensuring the bot loads reliably across devices.
  • Conversation design for public audience. Residents ask questions differently than city employees. This requires redesigning how the bot maps everyday language to city information, building escalation paths for each department, designing flows for common questions from the list the city is building, and handling edge cases gracefully (political questions, complaints, off-topic requests).
  • Content scoping and access controls. Ensuring internal documents from Phase 2 never surface in the public bot. The content boundary between what employees see and what residents see must be carefully defined and tested.
  • Legal, accessibility, and compliance. ADA compliance for the chat interface, disclaimer language, FOIA implications for bot conversations with the public, and clear boundaries for topics the bot will not address.
  • Branding and tone alignment. The bot represents the city to residents. Voice, personality, and response style need to align with how the city communicates publicly.
  • Multilingual considerations. Graceful handling of non-English input, even without full multilingual support.
  • Adversarial testing and monitored launch. Testing for misuse scenarios (attempts to get the bot to say something inappropriate), a soft launch with active monitoring, and first-week tuning based on real resident interactions.

Data source: Same public content from Phases 1 and 2 (internal-only documents are excluded from the public bot).

Estimated investment: $34,000

Phase 4: AI Voice Phone System

AI-powered phone system for incoming calls, common questions, and call routing.

View full details →

An AI-powered phone system that answers incoming calls, handles common questions via voice, and routes to the right person when needed. This replaces or supplements the traditional phone tree with a conversational AI experience.

Why Voice Is Different

Voice AI is fundamentally different from text. Every second of silence on a phone call feels broken. When a caller mishears or is misheard, there is no message to re-read. Callers interrupt, change their mind mid-sentence, and expect the system to keep up. Designing for voice requires handling barge-in (when callers talk over the bot), progressive error recovery, confirmation loops, and natural turn-taking. The quality bar is higher because a bad phone experience with the city feels worse than a bad chat experience.

Use Cases Identified from Gap Assessment

  • Utility billing questions
  • Compost site inquiries
  • General city information (hours, locations, departments)
  • Non-emergency public safety questions
  • Routing callers to the correct department or person

Scope

  • Platform research and evaluation. Evaluate multiple platforms (Microsoft Teams Phone with Copilot Studio, dedicated voice AI platforms like Bland.ai, Vapi, Retell AI), prototype against Sartell scenarios, and prepare a recommendation. Platform selection significantly affects scope and cost.
  • Voice conversation design. Each common question from the list the city is building needs its own voice flow with recognition variants, confirmation logic, follow-ups, and escalation paths. Call routing with warm transfer to the right department, passing context so the caller does not have to repeat themselves. Error recovery that progresses naturally: gentle rephrase, then alternatives, then human handoff.
  • Voice persona development. Pacing, tone, pause timing, and warmth. This is not just picking a voice from a dropdown. The persona needs to feel helpful and professional across every type of call.
  • Phone system integration. Connect to Sartell's existing phone infrastructure, configure SIP/trunk routing, number management, after-hours and holiday routing, and failover design (what happens when the AI platform goes down).
  • Knowledge base optimization for voice. Spoken responses need to be fundamentally different from text responses. Shorter, clearer, and designed for listening. Complex answers may need voice-to-email or voice-to-text bridges so callers can receive detailed information after the call.
  • Testing and monitored launch. Every flow must be listened to, not just read. Speech recognition accuracy across conditions (background noise, accents, cell vs. landline), latency testing, adversarial testing, and a two-week monitored launch period.

How It Connects

The knowledge base built and refined across Phases 1 through 3 feeds directly into the voice system. The "common questions" list the city is building supports this phase. The voice system uses the same AI engine with a conversation design optimized for spoken interaction.

Estimated investment: $34,000. These are early estimates that will be refined as Phases 1 through 3 progress and more is known about Sartell's phone infrastructure and call patterns.

Additional Workstream: Ticketing and Work Order System

Centralized ticketing and work order system with AI-powered intake, routing, and status tracking.

View full details →

The gap assessment identified a strong desire across nearly every department for a centralized ticketing and work order system with structured intake, workflow routing, status tracking, and a supervisor dashboard.

There are two distinct components to this:

The ticketing platform is the system where tickets live, workflows run, and dashboards display progress. This is workflow and application development that requires its own dedicated scoping.

The AI integration connects the chatbot as the front door to whatever ticketing system is in place. An employee tells the bot what they need, the bot collects the details through conversation, and a ticket is created automatically. Users can also ask the bot for status updates on existing tickets. This integration layer is part of the AI engagement and works regardless of which ticketing platform is selected.

The approach to the ticketing platform is currently being evaluated. Options include recommending a third-party platform (such as GovPilot, CivicPlus, or ServiceNow), partnering with a development firm, or building on Microsoft's existing tools (Power Apps, SharePoint Lists, Power Automate). A recommendation will be presented once the evaluation is complete.

The AI bot serves as the connective tissue between employees and whatever operational systems are in place. Whether the request comes through Teams, the website, or a phone call, the bot can create and check tickets on behalf of the user.
